# Insights

## Key Findings

- Non-emergency surgeries show the longest long-tail delay in the current output, with a 95th percentile wait time of 34.7 weeks.
- Specialist visits and selected diagnostic tests both reach 26.0 weeks at the 95th percentile, which suggests that access pressure is not limited to one service channel.
- Median waits are much lower than tail waits across all three service groups, which points to uneven patient experience rather than uniformly high delay.

## Business Implications

- A healthcare operations team should not rely only on median wait time because tail delays are materially worse for a subset of patients.
- Non-emergency surgery is the clearest candidate for escalation or capacity review in this dataset.
- Reporting both median and 90th or 95th percentile waits gives leaders a more realistic view of access risk.

## Recommended Story For Portfolio

This project shows how percentile-based reporting can surface hidden service pressure in Canadian healthcare access data. Instead of treating average performance as the whole story, the analysis highlights where long waits create a worse experience for patients who fall into the delayed tail.
